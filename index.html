The Problem

An involuntary movement
that paralyzes life

Parkinson's tremor doesn't stop movement — it contaminates it. It overlaps voluntary movement, making the simplest tasks impossible: holding a glass, writing, using cutlery.

⚡ Typical frequency: 4–7 Hz · Variable amplitude · Involuntary

Current treatments are invasive or insufficient

Deep brain stimulation requires neurosurgery. Medication (levodopa) loses effectiveness over time and causes severe side effects in 40% of patients.

No effective and accessible wearable solution exists

Existing devices have high latency, low adaptation range, or a price inaccessible to most patients in Latin America.

Tremor overlaps voluntary movement

The involuntary movement signal (high frequency) mixes with intentional movement (low frequency), making it nearly impossible to separate without active processing.

How it works

Sense. Decide. Act.
In less than 80 ms.

A closed-loop control system that differentiates tremor from voluntary movement and applies proportional mechanical resistance. Like a noise canceller, but for the body.

Step 01 — Sense
📡

Motion detection

The MMA7361 accelerometer measures acceleration on the X and Y axes in real time, capturing both voluntary movement and high-frequency Parkinson's tremor vibrations.

accel_x = analogRead(A0);
accel_y = analogRead(A1);
Step 02 — Decide
🧠

EMA filtering and classification

An Exponential Moving Average filter separates the signal: the slow component is voluntary movement; the difference between the original signal and the filter is the tremor (4–7 Hz).

low_pass = α × signal + (1-α) × prev;
tremor = current_signal - low_pass;
Step 03 — Act
⚙️

Active damping

The SG90 servo adjusts the tension of two antagonistic elastic bands. The greater the tremor amplitude, the greater the tension. One motor generates natural bidirectional resistance.

tension = Kp × tremor_amplitude;
servo.write(map(tension, 0, 100, 90, 160));
Hardware

Simple, affordable
and scalable

Low-cost, widely available components. The complete BOM costs less than $80 USD — designed for scalable manufacturing.

📡

Motion sensor

Analog MMA7361 accelerometer measures acceleration on X and Y axes. Direct analog output to the ADC, no additional protocol needed. Detects tremor frequency and amplitude.

MMA7361 Accelerometer
🤖

Microcontroller

Raspberry Pi Pico W processes the sensor signal, runs the EMA filter and calculates the control response each cycle. Built-in WiFi, low power, small form factor — ideal for a portable wearable.

Raspberry Pi Pico W · RP2040
⚙️

Mechanical actuator

SG90 servo located at the wrist, connected to a central pulley. Controls both elastic bands simultaneously in opposite directions. Bidirectional resistance with a single motor.

SG90 Micro Servo
🧵

Antagonistic transmission system

Double elastic band in antagonistic configuration with central pulley. When the servo rotates, one band tightens and the other loosens — natural and bidirectional tremor resistance.

Antagonistic elastic bands

Connect

Connect your SteadyHand
to the monitor

Follow these steps to link the Pico W to your WiFi and start visualizing tremor data in real time.

📶
Step 1

Connect to SteadyHand

Open your device's WiFi settings and connect to the network named SteadyHand. It's an open network — no password required.

→
🌐
Step 2

Open the setup page

Open your browser and go to http://192.168.4.1/setup. Enter your home or office WiFi name and password, then tap Save and connect.

http://192.168.4.1/setup
→
🔄
Step 3

Wait for restart

The device will save the credentials and restart automatically. After ~10 seconds it will be connected to your router with a new IP address.

→
📊
Step 4

Open the live monitor

Click the Live Monitor button in the navigation bar. Enter the IP shown in the device console and click Connect to start monitoring.
